Festival Waste Insights Report

Based on: 57 visitor conversations at Roskilde Festival 2026 · Themes identified: 6 · Date: July 2026

The headline

Visitors consistently report intention to take gear home and manage waste responsibly, but explicitly link follow-through to perceived effort and infrastructure availability. Post-festival exhaustion and low replacement costs drive gear abandonment despite acknowledged personal responsibility, while insufficient visible waste infrastructure forces visitors into littering despite stated commitment.

Insights

1. Personal Waste Responsibility And Agency~30% of people

Visitors acknowledge personal waste responsibility and intend responsible behavior when effort is minimized.

Visitors consistently frame waste management as shared responsibility, with personal accountability as the foundation. Most cite intention to take gear home, sort waste, and avoid littering -but explicitly link follow-through to perceived effort and infrastructure ease. A few visitors mention parental or maturity-driven motivation; others cite burden of pack-up as a realistic barrier to compliance.

What people said:

“The festival needs to make waste easy to get rid of and each person needs to get rid of it in a responsible way.”

“After a five-day festival, carrying a tent home feels like too much burden. Sorting boxes offer an easy solution so people don't have to carry stuff but also don't litter.”

“I'm going to take home all the cigarettes I smoke because I know they are really gross.”

2. Gear Sourcing And Sharing Practices~70% of people

Gear ownership and reuse norms drive take-home behavior more reliably than festival infrastructure.

Visitors with multi-year festival experience and owned gear consistently bring equipment home; those borrowing or renting show weaker commitment. Repeat attendees (10+ years) treat gear as durable assets worth protecting. Festival rental programs remove friction but do not yet shift abandonment rates among first-time or casual visitors.

What people said:

“I've been reusing my things for the past 15 years. So again, it's only the stuff that really breaks and I need new things for.”

“I will take everything home because I will use it again next year. I've done it all the years.”

“If people have invested in good quality tents, I think they would put more effort into bringing it back home and reusing it year after year.”

“I borrow everything. All my stuff was burned last year.”

3. Barriers To Responsible Waste Behavior~40% of people

Post-festival exhaustion overrides intention; visitors abandon gear despite acknowledging personal responsibility.

Visitors consistently cite physical and mental fatigue at pack-up as the primary driver of gear abandonment, even when they own the item and intend to take it home. Multiple respondents explicitly state tiredness after five to seven days prevents them from carrying tents, chairs, and mats despite recognizing this as wasteful. Reducing perceived effort at departure -through logistics solutions, rental systems, or accessible drop-off points -is necessary to convert stated intention into actual behavior.

What people said:

“After a seven-day festival with drinking, I'm not in good form to pack everything. It's too much of a hassle.”

“I feel really bad about not bringing my tent home, but I'm so lazy and tired after the festival I don't have the energy to carry all that shit.”

“If there are sorting boxes, you offer an easy solution so people don't have to carry all their stuff but also aren't horrible and littering.”

4. Gear Abandonment And Loss Concerns~50% of people

Gear abandonment is rational when visitors perceive pack-up effort as higher than replacement cost.

Visitors consistently abandon tents, chairs, and pavilions not from ignorance but from deliberate cost-benefit calculation. Multiple respondents cite fatigue, wet gear, and low replacement cost as justification: 'I'm so tired after the festival and I don't have the energy to carry all that shit home.' Broken or damaged gear triggers immediate abandonment; functional gear is retained only when perceived value exceeds effort. Festival systems that increase replacement cost (reinforced pavilions) or reduce pack-up friction (repair kits, hand-in points) shift this calculation.

What people said:

“Everything that isn't broken -implying broken gear is left by design, not accident.”

“I'm so lazy and tired after the festival that I don't have the energy to carry all that shit home -fatigue, not values, drives abandonment.”

“If people have invested in good quality tents, I think they would put more effort into bringing it back home and reusing it year after year -price and durability directly influence take-home behavior.”

5. Festival Infrastructure And Waste Systems~30% of people

Insufficient visible infrastructure forces visitors into littering despite stated responsibility.

Visitors consistently cite lack of trash cans and unclear sorting stations as the primary barrier to responsible waste disposal, even when personally committed. Multiple respondents report abandoning waste on-site because containers were absent, hidden, or inaccessible during peak pack-up hours. Infrastructure gaps override individual intention and create a false choice between carrying waste home and leaving it behind.

What people said:

“If I'm in the middle of a party, not seeing a trash can, I need to throw my beer can on the ground.”

“I spotted some containers every year, but there's not that many, and it's not really divided into plastic and trash.”

“If there are sorting boxes, you offer an easy solution for people to end, not have to carry all their stuff, but also not be horrible and litter.”

“I found it difficult outside the entrances to find trash cans. Focus on places to throw out trash at the entrances.”

6. Festival Culture And Engagement Dynamics~40% of people

Visitors blame festival infrastructure gaps while admitting personal apathy overrides intention.

Visitors simultaneously claim personal responsibility and cite systemic barriers -rental options, deposit systems, enforcement rules -as necessary to change behavior. Most acknowledge that without friction-inducing mechanisms, convenience and fun prioritization override pack-out intent. The gap between stated values and predicted behavior is structural, not motivational.

What people said:

“I think it's a personal responsibility, but the festival has a lot of responsibility in trying to engage people to take their things home.”

“When I'm at a festival, I don't really think about that shit. I just focus on having fun. But you're planting a seed in my mind to maybe be better.”

“People in the camps don't actually care too much about cleaning up after themselves. It's harder to engage them than to make a system that makes it harder to waste something.”

“I wouldn't have all these things if I hadn't planned for the festival. So definitely festival responsibility.”

3 experiments to consider

These are lightweight, low-risk actions the festival could try for next season or the current run. Each one addresses a pattern from the insights above.

Experiment 1: Set up staffed gear drop-off points during peak pack-up hours

The insight: Fatigue at departure is the stated blocker to pack-out, not lack of intention. A staffed drop-off eliminates the friction of carrying wet or heavy gear home by offering immediate, visible alternatives during the exact window when visitors are most tired.

Try this: Deploy two staffed collection stations at high-traffic exit points during the final six hours of the festival. Staff members accept tents, chairs, and mats without judgment and sort items into three bins: reusable (clean condition), repair-worthy (damaged but fixable), and disposal. Track volume and item types daily. Survey departing visitors at the station to measure whether proximity and staffing reduced abandonment compared to previous years. Run this for the full final day of the next festival.

Experiment 2: Introduce a five-pound gear deposit system for high-abandonment items

The insight: Visitors make explicit cost-benefit calculations: low replacement cost makes abandonment rational. A modest deposit on tents and chairs shifts the math without creating financial hardship. This directly addresses the deliberate calculation that drives abandonment.

Try this: Charge a refundable five-pound deposit on tents and chairs at entry or rental. Refund the deposit only if the item is returned to a designated collection point before departure. Clearly communicate the deposit amount and refund process in pre-festival materials and at entry. Track deposit redemption rates by item type. Compare abandonment rates for deposited items versus non-deposited items through site observation during pack-up. Run this for one weekend or a three-day festival window to test feasibility and visitor response.

Experiment 3: Place trash and recycling stations every fifty meters on main exit routes

The insight: Visitors abandon waste not from apathy but because containers are absent or hidden during peak pack-up. Proximity and visibility remove the false choice between carrying waste and leaving it. This is a direct infrastructure gap with immediate impact.

Try this: Map the three primary exit routes used during pack-up. Install clearly labeled trash and recycling bins every fifty meters along these routes, starting from the main camping areas. Use high-visibility signage with arrows pointing to the nearest bin. Staff bins during the final eight hours to prevent overflow and maintain accessibility. Count bin usage and measure waste volume collected from exit routes versus other festival areas. Survey departing visitors about bin visibility and accessibility. Implement for the full final day of the next festival.

How this analysis works

Insights are based on qualitative analysis of 57 short intercept interviews conducted at Roskilde Festival 2026. Rather than survey scores, this method surfaces patterns from what festival visitors actually said.

  1. Interviews are coded to surface recurring topics from each conversation.
  2. Codes are consolidated and grouped into thematic clusters across all interviews.
  3. Each cluster is analysed to produce a finding grounded in the source Q&A.
  4. Paraphrased quotes provide context while protecting anonymity.
