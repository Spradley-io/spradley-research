Team Insights Report

Based on: 12 confidential employee conversations · Themes identified: 6 · Date: July 2026

The headline

Employees report that managers avoid direct communication during uncertainty, leaving them without factual information about job security and questioning leadership credibility. The priority is establishing clear, fact-based communication protocols that separate reassurance from concrete answers during organizational change.

Insights

1. Work Preferences And Autonomy~50% of people

Employees thrive when given clear mandates and autonomy to execute without interruption.

High performers want explicit direction paired with unsupervised execution and minimal meeting overhead. Multiple respondents cite clarity of task and freedom from interruption as primary drivers of satisfaction and output. Removing friction from decision-making and protecting focus time directly enables the work these employees were hired to do.

What people said:

“Having a clear directive helps. I know what I need to do and why. The ambiguity is harder to sit with than the task itself.”

“The board is accessible when I need them, but they give me the latitude to do my job. I don't need hand-holding.”

“Phones get answered, orders go through, nobody calls a pointless meeting, and I leave at five. That's a good day.”

“Numbers are precise. They don't have feelings or opinions. I appreciate that.”

2. Job Performance And Competence~70% of people

Core job execution remains a reliable anchor when organizational clarity breaks down.

Employees derive genuine satisfaction from technical competence and task completion, independent of external chaos. Multiple respondents cite numerical work, sales execution, and straightforward task completion as sources of control and confidence. This intrinsic competence is a retention asset, but only if management stops treating it as a substitute for clarity and accountability elsewhere.

What people said:

“The numbers don't lie, and that part I can control. It's everything around it that feels uncertain.”

“When the board gives me a clear mandate, I can execute. That's the part I'm good at. It's the gray areas that drain you.”

“I spent three hours with him, bought him lunch, worked my magic, and walked out with a two-year contract. That's the Packman doing what the Packman does.”

3. Purpose And Motivation At Work~40% of people

Purpose splits sharply by role: strategic work and client relationships drive engagement; transactional tasks do not.

Engagement clusters around two distinct sources: strategic analysis and direct client interaction. Some employees find deep satisfaction in analytical work that shapes company direction; others are motivated primarily by deal closure and relationship building. A third group experiences their core tasks as instrumentally necessary but personally hollow, finding meaning only in interpersonal moments or external validation.

What people said:

“When the analysis is solid and the decision is sound, that's genuinely satisfying work.”

“Closing. That moment when you seal a deal - nothing beats it.”

“I sell paper. It pays my bills. That's what makes it worth doing.”

4. Management Communication And Trust Failures~80% of people

Manager avoidance of direct communication during crisis is eroding trust and productivity.

Michael's pattern of evasion, reassurance without facts, and deflection during the downsizing announcement has left employees unable to plan or focus. Multiple employees report asking direct questions and receiving vague answers, smiles, or topic changes instead. Sustained uncertainty about job security is now the primary driver of disengagement and the stated barrier to work quality.

What people said:

“I asked Michael directly if our jobs were safe. He smiled and said yes. That's not a guarantee. That's Michael filling the silence.”

“Just be straight with us. We're adults. If this branch is at risk, say so - don't give us vague reassurances that clearly aren't backed up by anything.”

“It felt like when someone says everything's fine in a voice that makes it very clear everything is not fine.”

“Day-to-day, it's a lot of managing around him rather than working with him. Every day there's something - a meeting, an announcement, a game.”

5. Distress From Workplace Incidents~40% of people

Management is failing to enforce policy against repeated misconduct, eroding trust.

Managers are treating workplace incidents as entertainment rather than disciplinary matters, allowing repeat offenders to operate without consequence. One employee documented three separate incidents of property destruction and received no formal action despite explicit requests. A second employee was subjected to a cruel prank that caused visible distress, then dismissed when raising concerns. This pattern signals to the team that misconduct is tolerated and management lacks authority.

What people said:

“Three times is not an accident, it is a campaign. I have documented each incident. Management needs to act.”

“Michael pulled this prank where he pretended to fire me - I actually started to cry before he told me it was a joke. Then when I tried to mention a raise, he told me to be professional.”

“It felt like when someone says everything's fine in a voice that makes it very clear everything is not fine.”

6. Workplace Integration And Belonging~20% of people

New employees lack cultural anchors and role clarity, forcing conformity over contribution.

Workplace integration fails at the point of entry. The new hire experienced social pressure to participate in an undefined situation without clear norms or permission to decline. Core tasks feel disconnected from personal meaning; connection depends entirely on individual coworker initiative rather than structural support.

What people said:

“I got pulled into something I didn't really ask to be part of. I didn't want to be the new guy who tanks the vibe on day one, so I went along with it.”

“Answering phones and sorting mail is fine, but what matters more is the people. Being the first face someone sees and making that feel okay -that part I actually care about.”

“It's my first day and I already heard this branch might get shut down. That's a lot to process before lunch.”

3 experiments to consider

These are lightweight, low-risk actions you can try in the next 2 to 4 weeks. Each one addresses a pattern from the insights above.

Experiment 1: Hold weekly downsizing Q&A with written answers

The insight: Employees cannot plan or focus because Michael's vague reassurances leave them unable to assess their own job security. Written answers create accountability and give people concrete information to act on.

Try this: Michael schedules a 30-minute weekly meeting for the next three weeks. Employees submit questions in advance via email. Michael prepares written responses addressing each question directly, including what he knows, what he doesn't know yet, and when he will have answers. He reads responses aloud in the meeting and distributes them in writing afterward. No topic changes or deflection allowed. Track which questions get asked repeatedly to identify the highest-anxiety areas.

Experiment 2: Document and resolve the three documented incidents this week

The insight: Repeat offenders operating without consequence signals that management lacks authority and misconduct is tolerated. Resolving the three documented incidents immediately restores credibility and shows the team that concerns are taken seriously.

Try this: Michael or HR reviews the three documented incidents of property destruction and the prank incident. For each incident, determine whether it warrants a formal warning, restitution, or other disciplinary action. Communicate the outcome to the affected employees and the broader team within five business days. Be specific about what happened, what action was taken, and why. If no action is warranted, explain that decision in writing. The goal is to demonstrate that incidents are investigated and resolved, not ignored.

Experiment 3: Assign each new hire a structured onboarding buddy

The insight: New hires experience social pressure without clear norms or permission to decline, and connection depends entirely on individual initiative rather than structure. A defined buddy role with explicit expectations removes ambiguity and creates a reliable entry point.

Try this: Identify one person on the team to serve as onboarding buddy for the new hire. Give the buddy a one-page checklist covering the first two weeks: introduce the new hire to three team members by name and role, explain the team's unwritten norms around breaks and collaboration, clarify which social events are optional versus expected, and schedule two 15-minute check-ins to answer questions. The buddy is not responsible for friendship, only for clarity and basic integration. Debrief with the buddy after two weeks to refine the checklist for future hires.

How this analysis works

Insights are based on qualitative analysis of 12 confidential employee conversations. Rather than survey scores, Spradley surfaces patterns from what people actually said.

  1. Conversations are coded to surface recurring topics from each interview.
  2. Codes are consolidated and grouped into thematic clusters across all interviews.
  3. Each cluster is analysed to produce a finding grounded in the source Q&A.
  4. Paraphrased quotes provide context while protecting anonymity.
